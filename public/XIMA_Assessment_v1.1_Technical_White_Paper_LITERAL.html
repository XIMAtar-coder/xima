1. Executive Technical Overview

XIMA Assessment v1.1 is a scenario-based professional profiling instrument designed to measure five cognitive-behavioural constructs across four professional domains. It serves as a decision-support tool for HR professionals, coaches, and enterprise talent teams. The instrument does not produce automated legally binding decisions and is not designed as a standalone hiring filter.

The assessment comprises 84 multiple-choice items and 8 open-response items (92 total), fully localized in English, Italian, and Spanish. Each item is mapped to one of five pillars via a fixed cyclic assignment. Scores are computed per-pillar, normalized to a 0–100 scale, and used to assign a XIMAtar archetype via Euclidean distance matching against prototype vectors.

Runtime integrity is enforced via djb2 hash verification of all locale question banks, preventing unauthorized modification of the frozen v1.1 item set.

2. Construct Framework — The Five-Pillar Model

2.1 Computational Power

Definition: The capacity to analyze structured information, decompose complex problems, identify patterns, and apply systematic reasoning to evaluate solutions.

Boundary: Does NOT measure IQ, academic intelligence, or mathematical ability in isolation. Measures applied analytical behaviour in professional contexts.

2.2 Communication

Definition: The ability to convey ideas clearly, adapt messaging to diverse audiences, navigate interpersonal dynamics, and manage information flow across stakeholder groups.

Boundary: Does NOT measure linguistic proficiency, language fluency, or presentation polish. Measures strategic communication behaviour under realistic constraints.

2.3 Knowledge

Definition: The capacity to acquire, evaluate, integrate, and apply domain-relevant information, including the ability to handle contradictory evidence and update beliefs.

Boundary: Does NOT measure factual recall, educational attainment, or credentialing. Measures knowledge management behaviour and epistemic flexibility.

2.4 Creativity

Definition: The ability to generate novel solutions under constraints, reframe problems, and produce actionable innovation when standard approaches are insufficient.

Boundary: Does NOT measure artistic talent or aesthetic judgment in isolation. Measures applied creative problem-solving under realistic professional constraints.

2.5 Drive

Definition: The capacity for sustained effort toward skill development, including self-diagnosis of weaknesses, deliberate practice, and adaptive learning behaviour ("Growth Velocity").

Boundary: Does NOT measure ambition, competitiveness, or work ethic as personality traits. Measures observable behavioural patterns of self-directed improvement.

4. Scoring Model

4.1 Item Scoring

All MC items use a fixed ordinal mapping: A = 0, B = 1, C = 2, D = 3. No item-level differential weighting is applied; all items within a pillar contribute equally.

4.2 Pillar Aggregation & Normalization

Raw pillar score = mean of mapped values for all items assigned to that pillar within the selected field. Normalized score = (raw mean / 3) × 100, yielding a 0–100 scale.

4.3 Open-Answer Integration

Open responses are scored by an AI rubric evaluator on a 0–100 scale. The blending formula is: final_pillar = (0.90 × mc_pillar) + (0.10 × open_score). open1 blends into Creativity + Communication; open2 blends into Drive + Knowledge.

Rubric criteria (conceptual): Specificity, measurability, self-awareness, actionable intent, contextual relevance. No proprietary model prompts are disclosed.

4.4 Drive "Growth Velocity" Model

Drive items are designed to measure the delta between current capability and improvement trajectory. Scoring emphasizes: (1) honest self-assessment of weakness, (2) concrete remediation strategy, (3) measurable progress indicators. This produces a "friction-based" score reflecting adaptive learning capacity rather than static self-report of motivation.

5. XIMAtar Assignment Logic

Each candidate's five pillar scores form a vector V = [CP, CM, KN, CR, DR]. Each XIMAtar archetype has a prototype vector Pi. Assignment uses Euclidean distance: d(V, Pi) = √Σ(Vk − Pik)². The XIMAtar with minimum distance is assigned. Ties are resolved by highest Drive score, then alphabetical order.

7. Validity Claims & Non-Claims

7.1 Claims

  • Construct separation across five cognitively distinct pillars
  • Scenario-based cognitive tension design reducing social desirability bias
  • Weakness-first item design on Drive pillar ("Growth Velocity")
  • Full trilingual parity (EN/IT/ES) with runtime hash enforcement
  • Cyclic pillar mapping ensuring balanced construct coverage

7.2 Non-Claims

  • XIMA is NOT a clinical diagnostic tool
  • XIMA is NOT a measure of IQ or general intelligence
  • XIMA is NOT a personality inventory (e.g., Big Five, MBTI)
  • XIMA is NOT predictive of job performance without contextual validation
  • XIMA is NOT free from all bias — design mitigates but does not eliminate
  • XIMA is NOT a standalone hiring decision system

8. Bias Mitigation & Ethical Design

Weakness-first design: Drive items require candidates to identify personal skill gaps and articulate remediation strategies. This reduces virtue-signaling by making the "right answer" depend on honest self-assessment rather than aspirational claims.

Anti-virtue-signaling: All MC options describe plausible professional behaviours. No option is transparently "correct," reducing the ability to game responses.

Cross-pillar bleed control: The cyclic mapping ensures each pillar is assessed by items distributed across the question sequence, preventing order effects from concentrating on a single construct.

Known limitations: Social desirability bias is reduced but not eliminated. Cultural interpretation of scenarios may vary. The instrument has not undergone large-scale differential item functioning (DIF) analysis.

Recommended monitoring: Track score distributions by language and demographic group. Flag items showing unexpected variance patterns for review.

10. Interpretation Guide

10.1 Reading Pillar Scores

Scores range 0–100 per pillar. High scores indicate preference for behaviours associated with that construct; low scores indicate preference for alternative approaches. Neither extreme is inherently "better" — interpretation requires role and context.

10.2 Avoiding Misuse

  • Do not over-interpret a single pillar score in isolation
  • Do not use as an elimination filter without structured interview
  • Always combine with contextual assessment (interview, work sample, references)

Pillar profiles support coaching conversations, team composition analysis, and targeted development planning. The Drive pillar specifically identifies self-improvement trajectory.

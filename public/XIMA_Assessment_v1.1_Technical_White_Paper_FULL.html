1. Executive Technical Overview

XIMA Assessment v1.1 is a scenario-based professional profiling instrument designed as a decision-support tool for HR, coaching, and talent development contexts. It measures a candidate's cognitive and behavioural tendencies across five orthogonal constructs ("pillars"), producing a pillar vector that is matched to one of 12 canonical archetypes (XIMAtar).

Target use cases: Development insight, team composition analysis, structured interview preparation, candidate pre-screening signal (not standalone filter).

Architecture: Fully client-side scoring with runtime i18n (EN/IT/ES). Assessment content is frozen via freezeGuard.ts with per-locale djb2 hash validation at application startup. Backend stores results in Supabase (assessment_results, assessment_answers).

2. Construct Framework

2.1 The Five-Pillar Model

PillarCore DefinitionDoes NOT Measure
Computational PowerSystematic analytical reasoning: decomposing complex problems, evaluating evidence, and constructing data-informed decisions under uncertainty.IQ, mathematical ability, or processing speed.
CommunicationAdaptive message construction: calibrating register, medium, and framing to achieve alignment across audiences with competing information needs.Extroversion, verbal fluency, or presentation charisma.
KnowledgeEpistemic rigour: evaluating source reliability, updating beliefs under conflicting evidence, and applying domain expertise under constraint.Volume of facts known, academic credentials, or memory capacity.
CreativityConstrained innovation: generating actionable solutions within real-world limitations (budget, time, compliance) rather than unconstrained ideation.Artistic talent, aesthetic sensibility, or divergent thinking tests.
DriveGrowth velocity: the observable rate at which an individual modifies their own behaviour after encountering friction, failure, or feedback — not static motivation or grit.Ambition, competitiveness, or self-reported persistence.

4. Scoring Model (Technical)

4.1 Item Scoring

All MC questions use a fixed ordinal mapping: A = 0, B = 1, C = 2, D = 3. There is no per-option differential weighting. Each question maps to exactly one pillar via cyclic assignment.

4.2 Normalization

pillar_score = (Σ answer_values for pillar) / (count × 3) × 100

Range: [0, 100]. Each pillar has 4–5 contributing items (Computational Power: 5 items; others: 4 items). The resulting 5-dimensional vector represents the candidate's profile.

4.3 Open-Answer Integration

Open responses are evaluated by an AI rubric engine (edge function analyze-open-answer) producing a score [0–10]. The open-answer contribution is blended at 10% weight:

final_pillar = 0.90 × mc_pillar + 0.10 × open_contribution

Blending targets: open1 → Creativity + Communication; open2 → Drive + Knowledge.

Rubric criteria (conceptual): Specificity of examples, measurability of outcomes, self-awareness and growth orientation, structural coherence (STAR/STAR-L pattern). The system does not penalise language quality.

4.4 Drive "Growth Velocity" Model

Drive questions are constrained to measure learning behaviour under friction: how quickly and effectively a candidate modifies their approach after encountering setbacks, repeated feedback, or skill gaps. This is distinct from static "motivation" or "grit."

5. XIMAtar Assignment Logic

Each candidate's pillar vector (5 dimensions, 0–100 each) is compared against 12 canonical prototype vectors using Euclidean distance. The closest prototype determines the XIMAtar archetype.

distance(candidate, prototype) = √(Σ (candidate_i − prototype_i)²) for i ∈ {1..5}

Tie resolution: When two prototypes have equal distance, a 25% bonus is applied when the candidate's dominant pillar aligns with the prototype's primary characteristic. If ties persist, the prototype with the higher Drive component is selected.

12 Prototypes: Lion (Executive Leader), Fox (Strategic Opportunist), Owl (Wise Analyst), Dolphin (Team Facilitator), Cat (Independent Specialist), Bear (Reliable Guardian), Bee (Productive Worker), Wolf (Tactical Team Player), Parrot (Expressive Communicator), Elephant (Long-Term Strategist), Horse (Reliable Driver), Chameleon (Adaptive Operator).

7. Validity Claims & Non-Claims

7.1 Claims

  • Construct separation: The five pillars are designed as orthogonal constructs measured through independent scenario-based items.
  • Cognitive tension design: All v1.1 items encode trade-offs between two legitimate strategies, avoiding clear "best" answers.
  • Reduced social desirability: The Weakness-First methodology reduces virtue-signaling by design. No option is transparently "correct."
  • Trilingual parity: EN/IT/ES versions are semantically equivalent with verified structural consistency.
  • Runtime integrity: Content immutability is enforced by hash validation at application startup.

7.2 Non-Claims

  • XIMA is NOT a clinical diagnostic tool.
  • XIMA is NOT a measure of IQ or cognitive capacity.
  • XIMA is NOT a personality inventory (Big Five, MBTI, etc.).
  • XIMA does NOT predict job performance without contextual validation.
  • XIMA is NOT free from all bias. Known limitations are documented in Section 8.
  • XIMA is NOT a standalone hiring decision system. It is a decision-support signal only.

8. Bias Mitigation & Ethical Design

  • Weakness-first design: Items prioritize identifying growth areas over confirming strengths, reducing defensive responding.
  • Anti-virtue-signaling: All options represent legitimate professional strategies. No option is designed to appear morally superior.
  • Cross-pillar bleed control: Each question is mapped to exactly one pillar. Scenario design minimises unintended measurement of adjacent constructs.
  • Known limitations: Fixed ordinal scoring (A=0..D=3) does not capture option quality differences. Cultural interpretation of scenarios may vary. The instrument has not yet undergone large-scale factor analysis.
  • Recommended monitoring: Periodic review of item-total correlations, differential item functioning (DIF) across languages, and distribution analysis of pillar scores by demographic group (when data permits).

10. Interpretation Guide (HR / Coaches)

10.1 Reading Pillar Scores

High scores (70–100): Candidate demonstrates strong, consistent application of the construct in scenario-based reasoning. Low scores (0–40): Candidate's default approach does not align with the construct's measured behaviour — this represents a development opportunity, not a deficiency.

Interaction effects: A candidate with high Creativity but low Computational Power may generate innovative ideas but struggle with systematic evaluation. These patterns are more informative than individual scores.

10.2 Avoiding Misuse

  • Do not use a single pillar score as an elimination criterion.
  • Do not compare raw scores across different professional fields.
  • Always combine XIMA signals with structured interviews and role-specific assessments.
  • Do not equate XIMAtar archetype with job suitability.

10.3 Development Use

XIMA results are most valuable in coaching conversations focused on: identifying the candidate's two weakest pillars (highest growth leverage), designing targeted development plans, discussing team composition and complementary profiles, and framing structured interview questions around observed patterns.
